OBJECTIVE

Increase the number of cancer patients who are properly matched to clinical trials.


Clinical trials are often a last option for cancer patients who are not responding well to standard treatments. However, matching patients to clinical trials successfully is a tedious, time-consuming, and controversial process for health care providers and patients.


The goal of this startup was to make this process easier using artificial intelligence to automate some of the tedious work and a user-friendly platform to connect patients to trials.

MY ROLE

Design Consultant & Front End Developer

TIMELINE

Jan - May 2017

TEAM

Designers (2), Business Owner, Physician, Developer

RESEARCH & ANALYSIS

A stressful and tedious matching process

To find a matching trial for a patient, oncologists and clinical research coordinator (CRC) nurses must navigate through hundreds of trials looking for matching diagnoses, specific criteria, and high quality practices. The documentation for one trial can be hundreds of a pages. With already busy schedules, this can be very difficult and time-consuming, especially for oncologists without a CRC nurse to support them.

Directed interviews

To understand the steps and pain points, we interviewed 16 different stakeholders in the process of matching cancer patients to clinical trials.

  • 7 Oncologists
  • 6 Clinical Research Coordinator (CRC) Nurses
  • 1 Cancer Patient
  • 1 Behavioral Oncologist
  • 1 MD/PhD Student

Affinity diagram

Interview findings were then consolidated to discover themes and patterns relating to:

  • Factors important to oncology patients
  • Learning about clinical trials
  • Deciding on appropriate trials
  • Criteria-matching process
  • Need for easy access to information

SYNTHESIS

Oncologists and research coordinators don't want to see all the trials--just the best ones for their patients.

From the interviews and affinity diagram, we pulled out key insights to inform our design approach. One major finding is that there are just so many trials--many of them with hundreds of pages and which are ultimately not a good fit for the patient.


Unfortunately, current systems do not make it convenient for oncologists and CRCs to find relevant trials for their patients. Trial criteria (for a patient to qualify for a trial) are becoming increasingly complex, intricate, and stringent.

"Lot's of patients come through everyday so it's hard to find time, unless you have one person doing that [searching for trials] every day."

Hematologist Oncologist

"Matching criteria is not easy. There are certain nuances where you'd need to talk to the investigator."

Hematologist Oncologist

It can be devastating for patients if matching with a trial doesn't work out.

Patients generally don't trust clinical trials because they aren't considered standard or best treatments, so they are already coming into the process wary. If they do consider participating in a trial, cost, travel, and time can be major barriers.


Given all the barriers to pursuing a trial, in the end patients can become very disappointed if the matching does not work out due to one criteria not being met, for example.

"Patients come in disappointed--they have already had bad experiences of previous treatment not working."

Behavioral Oncologist

DESIGN

How can we provide a quick glance of what really are the "best" trials for patients? How do we know what the best trials are?

Through interviews with physicians, research coordinators, and behavioral oncologists, we determined key information these providers looked for when considering trials.

Sketching

Concepts and layouts were sketched, and these ideas were shared with an oncologist to get feedback on what information would be important to emphasize.

Wireframes

Digital wireframes were then created and shared with additional oncologists.

Mid-fidelity design

Finally, mid-fidelity designs with color and styling added were created.

USER TESTING & FEEDBACK

Oncologists & CRCs have very limited time, so systems must be integrated, efficient, and easy-to-use.

These providers already use multiple different systems, so their feedback primarily focused on how this tool could easily integrate into their existing processes. For example, the tool could automatically pull patients' medical records so the information would not need to be re-entered. Of course, this would require multiple hurdles given the sensitivity of the information.

User testing

At each level of fidelity, we conducted think-aloud sessions with oncologists we had interviewed and also asked for general feedback.

"If it is a 3rd party system, no one will use it. It should be integrated into existing systems."

Radiation Oncologist

High-fidelity design

Designs were updated based on user-testing and made interactive in InVision.

Front-end development

An HTML framework for the application was created with styling and animations adding using CSS and JS. Implementation was discussed and planned with the back-end developer.

REFLECTION

Balancing client and user needs

Overall this project was a wonderful experience with learning how to be a better design consultant and communicate effectively with a client. Initially it was challenging to get on the same page, since the business perspective differed from my priorities as a designer. This involved many back-and-forth discussions, and learning from other design students and my advisor was a great help. Additionally, I was able to learn from the client and gain a clearer idea of how a startup works and the steps and challenges involved.
