CLASS PROJECT

Tween Social Coordination

Mobile app for tweens and parents to coordinate social activities

OBJECTIVE

Design a mobile app that allows tweens to begin to take ownership in setting up their own social activities while putting parents at ease?


Tweens (youths ages 8-14) want to be independent, but they have a lifetime of parents making all of the arrangements. Parents want their children to take responsibility for their own social events, but also want to stay in control and are concerned about the risks.

Interview Findings

  • Many plans tweens make are last minute — they often assume parents are free.
  • Last minute planning is stressful for parents as they have to shuffle things around or say no to kids.
  • Parents want their kids to be independent and coordinate their own social activities. However, parents also want to stay in control.
  • Tweens need transportion to socialize, but parents often do not have time or desire to drive or coordinate logistics.
  • The most popular apps among tweens are SnapChat and Instagram.

IDEATION

Reducing parents' stress while increasing tweens' independence

Ideation

As a team, we met and brainstormed a wide range of design solutions utilizing bodystorming and reframing.

Exploratory Scenarios

From 25 ideas, we selected and further developed 5 concepts. We decided to focus on the "Uber" for Minors concept.

  • Social Planning App: Calender of events that suggests activities for tweens or times when parents are available
  • Public Transit Simulation Day: Activity that increases parents' comfort about sending kids on public transit.
  • “Fill In For Me” Social Exchange for Moms: Moms share workload of driving tweens
  • Driverless Cars for Minors: Parents can track and communicate with driverless cars that transport tweens.
  • "Uber" for Minors: Tweens parents' need not necessarily be the ones to drive or coordinate. A pool of trusted drivers in the community on standby could help transport tweens in exchange for credits.

Storyboarding & Concept Evaluation

We presented the 3 most promising scenarios as storyboards to our class to elicit feedback.

Design Critique

Classmates and professors provided feedback on our final concepts.

  • Exchanging money or credits for rides seems too transactional and may lead to awkward social encounters
  • Generally, the concepts are missing the aspect of parental oversight and permission
  • Parents must approve the social event first — before transportation is even considered
  • Most parents do not currently feel comfortable with sending their tween in a driverless car

Re-Thinking Value Proposition

Responding to the feedback, we modified our concept.

Perhaps the app can utilize “social currency” instead of money or points to encourage social responsibility in tweens and the community. As tweens do good deeds for members of the community, other parents and trusted drivers can chip in to help transport them.

DESIGN

Final Proposal: Carma

Mobile app that allows tweens to get rides from pre-approved drivers in the community such as friends’ parents in exchange for doing good deeds and chores for these drivers.

Navigational Map: Tween's View

The goal for the tween’s view was to create a simple design that focused on two major functions of requesting rides and adding good deeds.

Navigational Map: Parent's View

For the parent’s view the major goal was to allow the parent to have flexibility and control. Therefore, we decided to have a tabbed view where the parent can quickly jump between their tween’s trip requests, record of good deeds, and list of approved drivers.

THOUGHTS AND FUTURE STEPS

Focus more on the experience of the parents, who are truly the primary users

While our audience of college students seemed to connect with the tween’s point of view more, it would make for a more powerful pitch if we centered our story around the parent’s challenges as most investors would relate more to this age-group. Additionally, the primary pain points affect the parents, not the tweens.

In a future iteration, it would be best to focus on the parents as the primary user and further develop the user experience for the third user group: drivers.

Final Pitch & Demo

For our final pitch to the class, we focused on the story of the personas, emphasizing the untapped resources. Carma eases the workload on parents and cultivate a sense of responsibility in tweens.
