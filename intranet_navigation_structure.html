NATIONWIDE INSURANCE

Intranet Navigation Structure

Information architecture for the updated employee intranet site.

OBJECTIVE

Redesign the structure of Nationwide's intranet navigation menu to be easier for employees to find the content they need.


Nationwide is creating a new intranet site that will gradually replace the current site. The primary navigation for both will be updated for a more seamless transition.

LIMITATION

Since the project scope was focused on just the primary navigation (rather than the site as a whole), value could only provided towards findability, not actual task completion.

MY ROLE

Information Architect and UX Lead

TIMELINE

May - Dec 2018

TEAM

Information Architect / UX Lead, Design Researcher, Business Leads (2)

PROCESS

Gathering existing and new knowledge to determine the best navigation structure

We first focused on understanding what employees need and want based on past research, analytics, and a card sort study. Then, we determined issues with the existing navigation. Insights from these informed our final recommended structure.

PAST RESEARCH & ANALYTICS

Employees primarily use the intranet for pay, benefit, and career information.

Repeatedly, we found evidence from past research and analytics that human resources and career information are most used and valued by employees at Nationwide.


Recommendation: Move human resources and career information upfront. Frequently used information should be easily visible and accessible.

Intranet survey

In a survey conducted in April 2018 of 2,041 Nationwide employees on their intranet experience, employees self-reported the employee directory and Associate Resources (pay, benefit, career information) as most frequently used and most valued.

Usability testing

A usability test conducted in 2013 also showed that employees at Nationwide primarily used the intranet for HR-type needs.

Web analytics

Web analytics from May 2017 to May 2018 showed that the primary navigation items with the greatest clicks were:

  1. Home (35%)
  2. HR-related items (31%)
  3. Career information (~13%)
  4. Business units (~3%)

CARD SORT STUDY

Understanding how employees would organize their intranet navigation

In order to get direct input from Nationwide employees today of how they would organize their intranet, we conducted a moderated open card sort with 12 employees of varying tenure, business units, and roles.

Process of consolidating navigation items

Content audit

From the 85 original navigation items, we looked for redundant items, outdated or rarely used items, and items that could be consolidated and narrowed this down to a more manageable list of 64 items.

Moderated open card sort

Participants created their own groupings and category labels for the primary navigation items. We also looked for what labels confused them.

Final 64 items used in card sort study

Employees generally grouped content into 4 primary categories:

  • About Nationwide: "Core values and policies. Where to go to learn about the company."
  • Company Information: "Areas of Nationwide. Info about the company that updates frequently."
  • Human Resources: "Associate-facing information, hire-to-retire, benefits and HR."
  • Resources/Tools: "Action-oriented resources, admin tools, quick links."

Recommendation: Base groupings of the navigation structure on these categories.

Dendrogram from UserZoom summarizing participant responses

Participants found the following items confusing: Associate Advantages, Concur, Experience Nationwide, Managing Budget & Finances, Marketplace, Brand, Learning Resources, Contractor Information, and Protect Nationwide.

CURRENT NAVIGATION

Pain points and usability issues in the current structure

We took a look at the structure of the current navigation and identified issues based on standard usability principles as well as user feedback from past research and the card sort study.

Recommendation: Select ordering based on employee needs. Design a menu that is more broad than deep. Large groups have reduced findability. Choose clearer labels.

RECOMMENDED STRUCTURE

Navigation structure proposed based on research, current usability issues, and business priorities

From past research and analytics, we determined how to best order the navigation items. Human resources and career information, the most valued and frequently used information, were moved upfront in the menu.


Overall groupings were based on participants' responses from the card sort study. Clearer labels were selected to reduce confusion and ambiguity. Finally, the menu was broken down into smaller, more even chunks to improve scanability and findability.

TREE TEST STUDY

Evaluating whether employees can find key information using the proposed structure

To evaluate the findability of our recommended structure and determine any problem areas, we conducted a remote tree test study with 110 Nationwide Associates of varying tenure, business units, and roles.

110

participants
(Nationwide employees)

14

key tasks tested

Prioritizing tasks

We determined tasks to test based on data we had on most common tasks as well as areas of concern (areas we had changed drastically from the current structure, areas that were more ambiguous or confusing, etc.).

Tree test method

Participants were given tasks and asked to click through the navigation structure to where they think they would find that task. We looked for whether participants clicked the correct primary and secondary categories as well as the actual page.

Participants went to the correct primary category for 14/14 tasks

Overall, a majority of the participants were able to successfully find the tasks given. Our key criteria for success was that participants went to the correct primary category, given that once a primary category is clicked in the navigation menu, a user will then see all of the pages within it.

Results from tree test study

Updates to structure based on the tree test

Some participants looked in "Associates" rather than "Workplace" for recognizing an Associate.


We therefore decided to move "Bravo!" (the tool used for naming particular employees who have done good work) under "Associates." We kept the general informational page, "Associate Recognition," under "Workplace."


Though other tasks had some incorrect responses, we decided not to make additional changes since a majority still went to the correct primary category, and the results did not indicate a more ideal location.

LEARNINGS

Use high-level summaries to get everyone on the same page

With so much data and information to go through, it was easy to lose sight of all the considerations and subconsciously make assumptions on what's best for employees.


I think it would have been helpful to create more high-level diagrams or summaries with my team members before starting to design the new navigation. This would put everyone on the same page and help ensure we were accounting for as much of this information as we could.

Consider navigation of the site as a whole rather than in parts

Because the project scope was limited, we only focused on the primary navigation menu. However, other parts of the site (e.g. the home page, hub pages, other Nationwide sites) are also part of how employees navigate their intranet. Without considering these, we risked potentially creating redundant navigation or not accounting for all the content available for employees.
